Want vs. Should

I came across this podcast courtesy of good ol' Thomas Randall (aka Randy). It occurred to me to just check up on what he was doing since I hadn't gotten a newsletter in a while and in one of his last blogs he mentioned the podcast. The general idea (to me) has been this: "should" is a toxic word that makes of think of desires as expectations, and in a way fosters a negative feeling towards them. The podcast does a better job explaining this but it highly resonated with me. I've spent the better part of my time self-studying doing so because it felt like I should be doing it. We're not gonna take this all the way, otherwise we'll end up wondering if that's the only reason I feel like I enjoy math. In any case I do think there is some truth to it (at least for me). I would like to cut the word out of my vocabulary and at least see what comes of it. The same goes for "ahorita". My problem is the following though: the immediate solution to the problem of shoulds is to follow your wants. For example, all the games and books in my backlog aren't shoulds, they were added to the list because I want to enjoy them, and it is only when I start to think that I should be playing/reading them that it turns into a responsibility I impose on myself and start to resent until I just feel "eh" towards it. Additionally, at the root of most of our behaviors is a want. I want to eat because I am hungry (or bored), I want to scroll Instagram because it is an immediately satisfying use of two minutes, I want to watch Dragon Ball because Krillin is fucking stupid for chasing the android p- I mean because of the inherent enjoyment I get from it. The podcast mentions the idea of tracing our wants as far back as possible and that if you can trace them back far enough to a "want that you don't actually want", then you'll naturally make the right choice. If I'm procrastinating a task, I can trace back the want of a different activity, to a want of immediate satisfaction, to a want of some kind of "small success", then realize that what I really want is the resolution of the task I'm procrastinating, and then go back to doing it and make the right in-the-moment decision. The problem with a reliance on want is that difficult things are hard to want. When I'm doing a plank, I don't want to feel pain, my immediate reaction is to let the plank go. I guess you could argue that the deeper want of gaining strength would override the immediate want to give up (surrender is an outcome far worse than defeat) but that kind of thought process doesn't occur for me automatically. Not yet at least - part of cutting out the s-word is to force myself to practice thinking in terms of what I really want when I do something. Another example I've lived this week is that I want to play Sekiro - and so I play Sekiro instead of pursuing other things that I know will contribute to my sanity later on. This last bit is what I really mean, listening to easy wants is much easier than listening to more challenging wants, so this practice can't be taken at purely face value. The last thing the podcast mentioned that stuck out to me though was to practice 30 minutes in the morning of just asking "who do I want to be in myself today" and I think I stand to gain from a morning routine like that. I have more to say on this, but I'd like more time to put it into practice and see what benefits and downsides come of it. On topic of a morning routine though...
