Okay. A closed set is the complement of an open set. Simple as that. Take $(0,1) \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, it's complement is $(-\infty, 0] \cup [1, \infty)$. That is a closed set. These things can be used to define a topology on a set just like we did with open sets actually, though in my head it feels a bit weird cuz you need a topology to already exist to have the idea of a closed set (with this definition at least). So, given a topological space, arbitrary intersections of closed sets are closed, as well as finite unions. I think interior/closure is usually talked about for subspaces so I'll answer for that - the interior of a subspace is the union of open sets contained within the subspace. Closure is the intersection of all closed sets containing the subspace. Closure is more talked about and important than interior but I don't have a full picture of why just yet. I need to keep an eye out for the answer to this as I keep reading.

Ok but all this talk of complements of open sets is a bit annoying - we can also define the closure of a set as the union of the set with all its limit points. Intuitively, the limit point of a set is one that is either in the set, or infinitely close to it. IIRC the formal definition is that it's a point such that every open set around it (i.e. every neighborhood around the point) intersects the set. That's why 0 is a limit point of (0,1).

A Hausdorff space is a topological space where every pair of points is contained in some disjoint neighborhood. The consequence of this is that point sets become closed. It is apparently wacky and unfun to try and make classifications of topological spaces that don't have this property, so we might work within its constraint later on. How does that condition accomplish that though? A weaker version of this is the $T_1$ axiom, which actually just states that consequence of the Hausdorff condition - that point sets are closed. This is weaker than the Hausdorff condition though and I still need to really understand why.

Speaking of things I still need to sit down and truly understand - how is the epsilon-delta definition of continuity I know and love from analysis the same as the general definition? That definition being: a continuous function $f: X \to Y$ is one where the preimage of open sets in $Y$ are open in $X$. Intuitively I think this can just be thought of as "open sets map to open sets" but formally, that condition is actually not equivalent (and probably weaker) than stating it with the preimage. Example I remember is mapping all of R to 0 - it's clearly continuous but every open set in R maps to a point set, which is not open. This also makes it clear that continuity really depends on the topologies of the domain and codomain.
