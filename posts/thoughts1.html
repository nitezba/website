Alternate title: help I'm fucking bored by early chapters in books

A "brief" note about this week's (it'll be the 6/11 one) summary: I'm starting work on the TIT part early, specifically D&F, due to a strong lack of motivation to do things I already feel like I've spent a good amount of time on in the past. In trying to do the problems, I find that I don't really need to prove group axioms for some set or another for the n'th time, and in trying to force myself I end up accomplishing nothing but frustration. In order to maintain accountability for Chapter 1 of D&F I've decided to do the following:

  1. The first metric of my actual comprehension of the material is my ability to communicate it. With this in mind, my hope is that by focusing on synthesizing the material in a coherent form right away (as opposed to slogging through notes I've essentially already taken) I can then compare what I come up with to the textbook to identify areas of weakness and/or straight-up lack of comprehension.
  2. The second applicable metric is my ability to do problems. A quick google search turned this up; I'll treat them as actual homeworks and post my solutions here. Trying to pick which ones to do on my own led to ambivalence and indeciveness; no reason to put in that effort when someone already has so I think following an existing homework structure provides the accountability and organization that I need.

As I wrote this I realized two things: 1) I think I accidentally stumbled upon a solid way of measuring comprehension in math. Definitely nothing novel in the grand scheme of things, but arriving at the conclusion myself makes it more valuable. And 2) I should post homework solutions.

Weasiling this in on Wednesday, but it's important to acknowledge that one of these metrics is definitely easier to apply than the others. Personally, I have a much easier time (and much more fun) synthesizing something I know and trying to explain it than going gung ho and doing actual problems. Both metrics are equally important and should be treated as such with the appropriate balance.
